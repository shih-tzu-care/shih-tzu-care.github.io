Published: 2026-05-22 · Shih Tzu Care

Bringing home a Shih Tzu puppy is a heartwarming experience, but it also comes with the challenge of housebreaking. Your new companion is a brachycephalic (flat-faced) breed with a small bladder and a stubborn streak. Consistency, patience, and a clear understanding of the Shih Tzu puppy potty training timeline will make the difference between a clean home and constant frustration. This guide provides a week-by-week roadmap based on veterinary science and real-world Shih Tzu experience.

Understanding the Shih Tzu Puppy Bladder

Shih Tzu puppies have very small bladders and a slow-developing ability to hold urine. On average, a puppy can control their bladder for one hour for every month of age, plus one hour (e.g., a 2-month-old can hold it for roughly 3 hours). For Shih Tzus, this timeline often stretches longer because of their relaxed nature and small size. Most breeders and veterinarians agree that reliable bladder control in Shih Tzus typically emerges between 4 and 6 months of age, though full maturity may take up to a year.

Why small breeds are harder to housebreak

Small breeds like the Shih Tzu have faster metabolisms and smaller bladders than larger dogs. A study on puppy behavior confirms that toy breeds often take longer to achieve consistent potty training success compared to larger breeds. You must adjust your expectations and schedule accordingly. There is no "magic age"—daily progress depends entirely on your consistency. For more insights on breed-specific challenges, check out our complete Shih Tzu care guide.

Critical week: 8 to 12 weeks old

This is the hardest phase. Your puppy needs to go outside every 30 to 60 minutes during the day, immediately after waking, eating, playing, and before sleeping. Puppy-proof a small space with easily washable floors. Crate training is highly effective for this age. According to the American Kennel Club, confining your puppy in a safe crate when unsupervised prevents accidents and teaches them to hold it. During this period, you'll also want to manage their environment carefully to avoid setbacks, as we discuss in our article on common Shih Tzu health issues.

This timeline is an average. Many Shih Tzu owners report that their puppy still has occasional accidents until around 8 to 10 months. Do not punish accidents—punishment leads to fear and hiding, which delays training. Instead, clean thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner to remove all scent markers. Setting Up a Routine That Works

The single most important factor in the Shih Tzu puppy potty training timeline is a rigid schedule. Dogs thrive on predictability, and Shih Tzus are no exception. Follow a daily routine where feeding, drinking, potty breaks, and play happen at the same times. A typical schedule for a 3-month-old Shih Tzu looks like this:

  • 7:00 AM – Wake up, immediate potty break
  • 7:15 AM – Breakfast (measured amount)
  • 7:30 AM – Potty break after eating
  • 9:00 AM – Potty break before crate time
  • 12:00 PM – Midday potty break and play
  • 3:00 PM – Afternoon potty break
  • 6:00 PM – Evening meal and potty break
  • 9:00 PM – Final potty break of the night
  • Midnight (if needed) – One last outing before bed

Use a crate or a small pen when you cannot supervise. The natural instinct of a dog is to avoid soiling their sleeping area, so a properly sized crate (just big enough to stand, turn, and lie down) helps them learn to hold it. If your puppy has an accident in the crate, the crate is too big or you left them too long.

Signs your puppy needs to go

Learn to read your puppy's body language. Pacing, circling, sniffing the floor, whining, or suddenly stopping play are clear signs that a potty break is needed. If you see any of these, immediately pick up your puppy (carefully) and carry them to the designated potty spot. Shih Tzu puppies are small and may not have time to make it outside on their own. For more tips on reading your puppy's cues, our about page discusses the importance of observation in training.

Crate Training and Nighttime Success

Choosing the right crate

A wire or plastic crate with a divider works best. Start with a small space so your puppy cannot sneak into a corner to eliminate. As they grow, move the divider to enlarge the space. Place the crate in your bedroom initially—this lets you hear whining and take them out promptly. Shih Tzus are companion dogs and often sleep better near their owner. The right crate setup is also a key element of our complete Shih Tzu care guide.

What to do when your puppy cries at night

Do not ignore a crying puppy. They are telling you they need to potty. Take them out on a leash to the designated spot, use a quiet command like "go potty," give a tiny treat if they go, and immediately return them to the crate. No playtime. Nighttime accidents often happen because you waited too long. Set alarms every 2 to 3 hours for the first few weeks. The VCA Hospitals note that exercise during the day also helps your puppy sleep more soundly at night, reducing the urge to eliminate from restlessness. This approach aligns with the positive training philosophies we explore on our about page.

Common Setbacks in Shih Tzu Potty Training

Even with a perfect plan, setbacks happen. Here are the most common reasons Shih Tzu puppies regress:

  • **Over-excitement:** When greeting people or playing, some puppies lose control. This is called submissive or excitement urination. Stay calm and avoid loud greetings.
  • **Fear or anxiety:** Loud noises (thunder, fireworks) or new environments can cause accidents. Build your puppy's confidence slowly.
  • **Medical issues:** Urinary tract infections (UTIs) are more common in small breeds. If your puppy is having frequent accidents after a period of success, consult your vet.
  • **Inconsistent schedule:** Even one day without a strict routine can set you back a week of progress.

If you notice blood in urine, straining, or accidents in their sleep, contact your veterinarian immediately. This could signal a UTI or other medical problem requiring treatment. There is a helpful article on common Shih Tzu health issues on our blog that discusses this further.

The Role of Diet and Hydration

What you feed your puppy directly affects their potty schedule. High-quality puppy food with consistent fiber content helps regulate bowel movements. Free-feeding (leaving food out all day) is a terrible idea for potty training. Instead, feed measured meals at set times twice a day (three times for the first few months). Water intake should be controlled but never restricted during the day. Offer fresh water after meals and after potty breaks. Remove water 1 to 2 hours before bedtime based on your puppy's age.

Meal PracticeEffect on Potty Training
Scheduled meals (twice daily)Predictable elimination times
Free feedingRandom bowel movements, harder to predict
Frequent small meals for puppies under 4 monthsStabilizes digestion and reduces stomach upset

Always have water available during the day, but monitor your puppy’s intake. Overhydration can lead to more frequent accidents. If your puppy drinks excessively, it may indicate a health issue; speak to your vet. For additional scheduling tips, see our full potty training timeline article.

Using treats and rewards effectively

Positive reinforcement is the fastest way to train a Shih Tzu. Use tiny, high-value treats (like bits of cooked chicken or freeze-dried liver) immediately after your puppy eliminates in the correct spot. The reward must happen within seconds, not minutes. Praise in a high-pitched, happy voice. Avoid punishment for accidents indoors—it creates fear and anxiety, which makes training take longer. Instead, interrupt the accident by clapping (don't shout) and take them outside immediately. If they finish outside, reward them. When Potty Training a Shih Tzu is Taking Too Long

Some Shih Tzu owners struggle for a year or more. If your puppy is 8 months old and still having accidents every day, it is time to reassess your approach. First, rule out medical issues with a vet check. Second, consider that you may be expecting too much too soon. Reduce the time between potty breaks. Go back to basics: crate training, supervision, strict schedule, and immediate rewards. Third, consider if your puppy has had access to areas where they had previous accidents. The smell of urine (even if you can't detect it) can trigger them to go again. Use an enzymatic cleaner thoroughly. You can also try a bell training system—teach your puppy to ring a bell hanging by the door whenever they want to go out. This gives them a clear way to communicate. For more tips, read our full potty training timeline article.

Accidents Happen: How to Clean Up Properly

If your puppy eliminates on the carpet, cleaning is not just about removing visible mess. Dogs have a far superior sense of smell. If they can smell urine, they will think that spot is a bathroom. Use an enzymatic cleaner specifically designed for pet urine. Do not use ammonia-based cleaners—they smell similar to urine and can attract your puppy back to the same spot. Blot up as much urine as possible with paper towels, then apply the cleaner. Let it sit for the recommended time (often 10–15 minutes) before blotting again. For hard floors, use a vinegar and water solution after the enzymatic cleaner. Follow this with a thorough rinse. Proper cleaning is a step we highlight in our complete Shih Tzu care guide as well.

Helping Your Shih Tzu Succeed Indoors (When Necessary)

During extreme weather or if you live in an apartment, you may need an indoor potty solution. Some Shih Tzu owners use a patch of real grass (available from pet supply companies) placed on a tray. This provides a natural surface for your puppy and can be a bridge to outdoor potty training later. Others use training pads. A word of caution: training pads can confuse some puppies because they teach them that it is okay to eliminate indoors. If you intend to eventually transition to only outdoor potty breaks, use the pad only as a temporary measure. Place the pad near the door and gradually move it outside over a few days. For additional insights on indoor training, see our contact page if you need personalized guidance, or explore the sitemap for all articles.

Should you use crate training with pads?

Yes, you can. Many owners create a small pen attached to the crate. The crate itself is a no-potty zone, but the pen area has the pad or grass patch. This gives your puppy a safe alternative and prevents accidents in the crate. The downside is that it can slow down the transition to outside-only potty training. Most trainers recommend eliminating pads as soon as possible, typically around 4 to 5 months of age. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it typically take to potty train a Shih Tzu puppy?

Most Shih Tzu puppies achieve consistent daytime bladder control between 4 and 6 months, though some take up to a year. Full maturity can take longer than for larger breeds.

Why does my Shih Tzu puppy regress and start having accidents again?

Common causes include over-excitement, fear, medical issues like UTIs, inconsistent schedules, or a change in environment. Rule out medical causes first, then re-establish a strict routine and limit access to areas where past accidents occurred.

What is the best age to start potty training a Shih Tzu?

Start as soon as you bring your puppy home, typically around 8 weeks old. The earlier you establish a routine, the faster the training progresses.

How often should I take my Shih Tzu puppy outside?

For a 2-month-old, take them out every 30–60 minutes. For a 3-month-old, every 2 hours during the day. Always take them out immediately after waking, eating, playing, and before sleeping.

Is crate training safe for Shih Tzu puppies?

Yes, when done correctly. A properly sized crate provides a safe haven and uses your puppy's natural denning instinct to discourage soiling. Never use the crate as punishment.

Can I use training pads for my Shih Tzu?

Yes, as a temporary indoor solution. However, pads can delay outdoor training. If using pads, place them near the door and gradually move them outside over a few weeks.

What should I do if my puppy has an accident in their crate?

First, assess the size of the crate. If it is too large, your puppy can use one corner as a bathroom. Clean the crate thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner. Also, check your schedule—you may be leaving them too long.

My Shih Tzu is 6 months old and still peeing inside. What now?

First, visit your vet to rule out a UTI or other medical issue. Then, go back to basics: strict schedule, crate supervision, frequent breaks, and immediate rewards. Do not allow unsupervised access to the entire house.

Do male Shih Tzu puppies mark indoors?

Yes, some neutered and unneutered males may urine mark, especially when they reach sexual maturity (around 6 months). This is a behavioral issue unrelated to potty training. Consult a trainer if marking persists.

Is it okay to use punishment for potty accidents?

No. Punishment (such as yelling or rubbing their nose in it) creates fear and can lead to hiding, submissive urination, or aggression. Positive reinforcement is more effective and builds trust.

How can exercise help with potty training?

Regular exercise helps regulate your puppy's metabolism and bowel movements, making potty breaks more predictable. It also reduces anxiety and promotes better sleep at night, as noted by VCA Hospitals.
