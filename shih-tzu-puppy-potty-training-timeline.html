Shih Tzu Puppy Potty Training Timeline

Updated 2026 — The honest week-by-week timeline every Shih Tzu owner needs. Why accidents happen longer than expected, when real progress arrives, and what actually works for this stubborn, charming breed.

You cleaned up three puddles before 9 a.m. You praised every outdoor trip. You set an alarm for 2 a.m. to take the puppy outside. And then you stood in the kitchen watching your Shih Tzu confidently squat on the rug you just washed. If this sounds familiar, you are not failing. You have simply met one of the most notoriously slow breeds to potty train—and no one prepared you honestly for the Shih Tzu puppy potty training timeline.

This article will not sugarcoat the timeline or pretend results arrive in two weeks. Instead, it gives you the real picture: when to expect what, why this breed takes longer than most, and what strategies close the gap between "hopeless" and "finally got it."

Why Shih Tzus Are Genuinely Harder to Potty Train

Before the timeline, you need to understand why this breed tests owners so thoroughly. Shih Tzus were bred for centuries as palace lapdogs in China—not outdoor working dogs. They have almost no natural instinct to go far from their resting spot. That means the drive to seek a specific outdoor toilet area simply is not hardwired into them the way it is in gun dogs or herding breeds.

On top of that, their physical development lags. A Shih Tzu puppy's bladder sphincter muscle is not fully mature until around 6 months of age. Before that point, your puppy is not choosing to have accidents—they genuinely cannot hold the urge longer than a few minutes after it strikes. This is not stubbornness; it is anatomy. Many owners mistake physical inability for deliberate defiance, and that misunderstanding leads to frustration on both sides.

Finally, Shih Tzus are sensitive dogs. Harsh corrections—even a sharp "no"—can make them anxious about going in front of you at all. Some dogs begin sneaking behind furniture to eliminate, making training dramatically harder. The care fundamentals for Shih Tzus always start with this: gentle consistency wins over force every single time.

According to the AKC's potty training guidance, small breeds typically take 4–6 months longer than medium or large breeds to achieve reliable housetraining. With Shih Tzus, many owners report the process taking 6–12 full months before accidents drop to near zero.

The Week-by-Week Shih Tzu Potty Training Timeline

This timeline assumes you bring your puppy home at 8 weeks and begin training on day one. Expect variation—every dog is different—but these milestones are what realistic progress looks like.

The Bladder Problem No One Talks About

Here is the fact that changes everything: a Shih Tzu puppy at 8 weeks can hold its bladder for roughly 30 to 45 minutes maximum. The general rule of thumb is one hour per month of age, but in small breeds the real number is often even shorter. A 10-week-old puppy who has been playing for 20 minutes may already be on borrowed time.

This is why accident prevention in the early weeks is less about training and more about management. If your puppy cannot physically hold the urge, no amount of correction will stop the accident. Your job is to catch them before the urge overcomes their capacity—by taking them outside often enough that the urge never builds to the breaking point.

Crate Training: The Tool That Changes Everything

If you are struggling with the Shih Tzu potty training timeline and have not yet introduced a crate, this section is for you. Dogs instinctively avoid soiling their sleeping space. A correctly sized crate—just big enough for your puppy to stand, turn, and lie down—uses that instinct to teach bladder control passively.

The crate should never be used as punishment. It is a den: a safe, warm, calm space where your puppy rests between outdoor trips. When your Shih Tzu is in the crate, they hold on. When you open the crate, you carry them directly outside. The moment they eliminate outdoors, you celebrate with high-value treats and genuine excitement. Over hundreds of repetitions, the neural pathway forms: outside is where elimination happens.

Crate Training Schedule for Shih Tzu Puppies

  • Wake up: Take outside immediately (carry if needed)
  • After eating: Outside within 5–10 minutes
  • After play: Outside within 15 minutes
  • After naps: Outside immediately upon waking
  • Before bed: Last trip outside as close to lights-out as possible
  • Night trips: Every 3–4 hours for puppies under 4 months

The VCA Animal Hospitals crate training guide recommends that puppies never spend more than their maximum hold time in a crate. If they must stay longer, arrange for a midday break from a trusted person or a dog walker.

Common Mistakes That Add Months to the Timeline

Most owners who report their Shih Tzu was "impossible to train" made one or more of these mistakes. Recognizing them is the fastest way to course-correct.

  • Too much freedom too soon. Giving a puppy access to the whole house before they are reliable is like asking a toddler to use the toilet and leaving them unsupervised in a large building. Limit space until the habit is solid.
  • Inconsistent schedules. Shih Tzus need predictability. Feeding at random times, taking outside at random intervals, and skipping trips on busy days all delay progress significantly.
  • Punishing accidents after the fact. If you find a wet spot on the rug and show it to your puppy, they do not connect the punishment to the act. They connect it to you—and become fearful. Clean it up silently with an enzymatic cleaner and move on.
  • Inadequate cleanup. Dog noses detect residual urine odor that humans cannot smell, even after regular cleaning. Enzymatic cleaners (like Nature's Miracle) are the only effective solution. If a spot is not fully neutralized, your puppy will return to it.
  • Pee pads creating confusion. Pee pads can be useful in some situations, but for many Shih Tzu puppies they teach that eliminating indoors on absorbent surfaces is acceptable—which directly undermines outdoor training. If you use them, plan a deliberate transition strategy.

Health Issues That Affect Potty Training Progress

If your Shih Tzu seems to be going backwards after making progress, or has unusually frequent accidents even on a good schedule, a veterinary visit is warranted. Several health issues common to the breed can directly impact bladder control.

Urinary tract infections (UTIs) are frequent in Shih Tzus, especially females. A UTI creates a constant urge to urinate even when the bladder is nearly empty—making bladder control impossible regardless of training. Symptoms include straining, small frequent urinations, and sometimes blood in the urine.

Bladder crystals or stones can also cause frequent accidents and discomfort. Both conditions require veterinary diagnosis and are completely treatable once identified.

Beyond bladder issues, Shih Tzus are prone to certain health conditions that can affect their overall behavior and stress levels. Dogs experiencing eye discomfort or respiratory difficulty may regress in training simply due to the distraction of physical discomfort. Our guide to Shih Tzu eye problems covers the most common conditions affecting this breed, many of which are easily treated when caught early. Similarly, brachycephalic breathing problems affect a significant portion of Shih Tzus and can cause anxiety that shows up as house training regression.

What Success Actually Looks Like at Each Stage

One reason owners feel discouraged is that they expect a clean switch from "lots of accidents" to "no accidents." The reality is gradual reduction, not a single turning point.

At 10 weeks, success means your puppy had 10 accidents today instead of 14. At 16 weeks, success means you caught the sniff-and-circle signal twice and made it outside both times. At 6 months, success means only one accident in the last three days. At 9 months, success means you cannot remember the last accident.

Each of those is real progress. Measure it against your starting point, not against an imagined ideal timeline from a breed that matures faster.

Grooming and Potty Training: The Surprising Connection

One factor that owners rarely consider: coat hygiene directly affects potty training progress. A Shih Tzu with long fur around the rear end may have urine or feces matting into the coat, making them uncomfortable and anxious—and anxious dogs lose bladder control more easily. Keeping the sanitary areas trimmed short is not just hygiene; it is part of a successful training environment.

Our guide on how often to groom a Shih Tzu covers facial and sanitary trim frequency. Most owners find that a short sanitary trim every 3–4 weeks keeps this issue under control and makes cleanup after accidents significantly easier.

When to Expect Full Reliability

The honest answer most owners need to hear: expect 6–12 months from your start date for full daytime reliability, and 10–14 months for full nighttime reliability. These numbers are supported by ASPCA house training guidelines which note that small breeds with stubborn temperaments are often in the longer range of this window.

That timeline sounds long—and it is. But "not yet reliable" at 5 months does not mean "never reliable." It means you are in the middle of the normal window for this breed. The owners who give up at month 4 are the ones who miss the success that was coming at month 7.

Keep detailed notes if it helps. Many owners track the day of the week, the time, and the location of accidents for a few weeks. Patterns almost always emerge—a specific spot, a specific time of day, or a specific trigger like guests arriving. Once you see the pattern, you can intervene before it happens.

Interactive FAQ: Your Questions Answered

How long does it take to potty train a Shih Tzu puppy?

Most Shih Tzu owners achieve reliable daytime house training between 6 and 9 months of age, with full nighttime reliability following 1–3 months later. Smaller individuals or those with health issues may take slightly longer. Consistent schedules and crate training shorten the timeline significantly.

Why does my Shih Tzu keep having accidents even after months of training?

The most common causes are: incomplete enzymatic cleaning of previous accident spots, too much unsupervised freedom too soon, inconsistent schedules, or an underlying health issue like a UTI. If accidents are very frequent, very small in volume, or accompanied by straining, see your vet to rule out a bladder infection or stones.

Should I use pee pads for my Shih Tzu puppy?

Pee pads can be helpful in high-rise apartments or during very cold weather when outdoor trips are difficult. However, they carry a real risk of teaching your puppy that eliminating indoors is acceptable. If you use them, plan a gradual transition: move the pad toward the door, then just outside the door, then outside entirely over several weeks.

What should I do when I catch my Shih Tzu having an accident?

Calmly interrupt with a neutral word ("outside!"), pick them up immediately, and carry them outside to their designated spot. If they finish outside, reward enthusiastically. Do not shout, rub their nose in it, or show anger—this creates anxiety and makes future training harder. Clean the indoor spot with an enzymatic cleaner.

My Shih Tzu was trained and now is having accidents again. Why?

Regressions are common during teething (3–6 months), adolescence (6–12 months), after changes in routine, during illness, or if a new pet or family member joins the household. Rule out UTIs first with a vet visit, then go back to basics: shorter outdoor intervals, more supervision, and temporarily limiting access to problem rooms.

Is a Shih Tzu harder to potty train than other small breeds?

Yes, Shih Tzus are generally considered one of the more challenging small breeds to house train, alongside Dachshunds and Bichon Frises. Their lapdog heritage means they have a weaker natural drive to eliminate away from their resting space. They also respond poorly to pressure, which can backfire with inexperienced trainers. Patience and positive reinforcement consistently outperform any forceful approach with this breed.

Final Thoughts: The Training You Do Now Pays Off for 15 Years

A Shih Tzu's average lifespan is 10–16 years. The weeks you invest in consistent potty training right now are the foundation of a decade and a half of living with a clean, calm, happy dog. The frustration you feel at month 3 or 4 is not the final chapter—it is chapter three of a very long, very rewarding book.

Stay consistent with your schedule, use the crate as a positive tool, clean accidents completely, and give your puppy the physical time their bladder needs to mature. If you suspect a health issue behind the accidents, visit your vet before assuming it is a training problem. And keep coming back to our Shih Tzu care guide as your puppy grows—there are new challenges at every stage, and you do not have to figure them out alone.
