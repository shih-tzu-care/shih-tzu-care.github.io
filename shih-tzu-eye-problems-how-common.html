Few things are as endearing as the dark, round eyes of a Shih Tzu gazing up at you. However, those same prominent eyes are also the breed’s most vulnerable feature. If you own a Shih Tzu or are considering adding one to your family, understanding the prevalence of Shih Tzu eye problems how common they are is essential for responsible care.

Shih Tzus are brachycephalic (short-nosed) dogs with shallow eye sockets, making their eyes more exposed than those of many other breeds. This anatomical predisposition means eye issues are not a possibility—they are a statistical probability. According to veterinary epidemiological studies cited by PubMed Central, Shih Tzus are among the top three breeds most commonly diagnosed with ophthalmic disorders in veterinary practice. This article provides a data-driven, owner-focused examination of just how common these problems are, what you can do, and when to seek emergency care.

The Anatomy Behind the Vulnerability

To understand why eye problems are so common in the Shih Tzu, you must first look at their unique head structure. The breed standard calls for a large, round head with a short muzzle. While this gives them their signature "chrysanthemum face," it creates structural challenges.

Shallow Orbits and Exophthalmos

The bony eye sockets (orbits) of a Shih Tzu are notably shallow. This condition, known as exophthalmos, means the eyeball sits proportionally more forward than in a non-brachycephalic breed like a Labrador Retriever. Data from the Cornell Riney Canine Health Center indicates that breeds with exophthalmos have a significantly higher incidence of corneal exposure and subsequent injury. Because the eyelids cannot close fully over the protruding globe, the cornea is constantly at risk of drying out and being scratched.

Cleft Palate and Tear Duct Issues

The facial folds that give Shih Tzus their charm also block the drainage of tears. A study published in the Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association and cited by Merck Veterinary Manual found that inherited tear duct malformation is common in brachycephalic breeds, leading to chronic epiphora (excessive tearing). This moisture creates a breeding ground for yeast and bacteria, compounding the risk of secondary infections.

Prevalence of Shih Tzu Eye Problems: The Numbers

So, how common are Shih Tzu eye problems? The data is sobering. A large-scale epidemiological study examining breed-specific health risks found that Shih Tzus are over four times more likely to be diagnosed with a corneal ulcer compared to the average mixed-breed dog. Below is a breakdown of common conditions and their relative risk.

Most Common Eye Conditions in Shih Tzus

While the list of potential problems is long, a handful of conditions account for the vast majority of veterinary visits related to Shih Tzu eyes. Understanding these specific issues is the first step toward prevention and early treatment.

Corneal Ulcers

A corneal ulcer is essentially a scrape or hole on the surface of the eye. Given the prominent position of the Shih Tzu eye, they are incredibly prone to trauma from rough play, brushing against furniture, or even from their own long eyelashes (distichiasis). A study from the AKC Canine Health Foundation notes that the breed’s high incidence of "dry eye" compounds the risk, as a dry cornea is far more susceptible to ulceration.

Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca (KCS / Dry Eye)

Dry Eye is an autoimmune condition where the tear glands produce an insufficient amount of the watery layer of tears. Without adequate tears, the cornea becomes inflamed and scarred. The Merck Veterinary Manual reports that while all dogs can get KCS, Shih Tzus and other brachycephalic breeds are overrepresented in diagnostic data. This is a chronic, life-long condition that requires daily medication.

Proptosis (Eye Popping Out)

Perhaps the most frightening emergency for any Shih Tzu owner is proptosis, where the eyeball is displaced from the socket. Because the eyelid opening is relatively large and the socket is shallow, even mild trauma—such as a quick yank on a leash or a fall from the couch—can cause the eye to pop forward. This is a veterinary emergency that requires immediate intervention to save the eye and vision.

Recognizing Symptoms: When to Look Closer

Early detection of Shih Tzu eye problems can mean the difference between simple medication and major surgery or vision loss. You should perform a quick visual examination of your dog's eyes daily.

Red Flag Signs

Any of the following symptoms warrant a prompt veterinary visit:

  • Squinting or Blepharospasm: If your Shih Tzu keeps one or both eyes partially closed, it is a strong indicator of pain. This is the most common sign of a corneal ulcer.
  • Excessive Tearing or Discharge: While tear staining is cosmetic, a sudden increase in thick, yellow, or green discharge indicates infection.
  • Rubbing the Face: Pawing at the eye or rubbing the face on the carpet is a sign of irritation or pain.
  • Cloudiness or Color Change: A blue or white haze on the cornea can indicate severe edema or a deep ulcer. A red spot often indicates a bleeding ulcer.
  • Bulging of the Eye: If one eye appears to be protruding more than the other, it requires immediate evaluation.

The "At-Home" Check

To assess the situation between vet visits, you can conduct a simple Schirmer tear test once prescribed by your vet, but at home, you can look for subtle signs. Gently pull down the lower eyelid to check for redness or swelling. Look for any foreign objects like a hair or grass seed. If your dog is squinting and you cannot see a foreign body, do not attempt to touch the cornea further—seek professional help. More detailed self-care management steps can be found on our blog.

Prevention: Management Strategies Every Owner Should Know

While you cannot change your dog's anatomy, you can dramatically reduce the risk of injury and disease through consistent management. Preventing Shih Tzu eye problems is an active, daily responsibility.

Humidification and Environmental Control

Dry air exacerbates Dry Eye. If you live in a dry climate or use heating/air conditioning, consider using a humidifier in the rooms where your dog spends the most time. This helps maintain a healthy tear film. Avoid dusty environments and keep your dog away from dry, sharp grasses like foxtails, which can easily lodge under the third eyelid.

Grooming and Hygiene Protocols

This is arguably the most important aspect of daily care. The hair on a Shih Tzu's face grows inward and can scratch the cornea. Trimming the hair between the eyes (the "topknot") short is essential. Further guidance on proper grooming techniques is available on our care page.

Additionally, regularly clean the facial folds with a soft, damp cloth to prevent moisture buildup that leads to secondary bacterial infections. Use a tear stain remover specifically formulated for dogs, not hydrogen peroxide, which can be irritating.

Diagnostic and Treatment Pathways

If you suspect your Shih Tzu has an eye problem, your veterinarian will use a series of straightforward diagnostic tests. Understanding these can help you feel more prepared for a vet visit.

Treatment Options

Treatment varies widely based on the condition. For Dry Eye, life-long application of cyclosporine (Optimmune) or tacrolimus is standard. For simple corneal ulcers, topical antibiotics and atropine for pain relief are used. For deep or non-healing ulcers (indolent ulcers)—which are common in Shih Tzus—a surgical procedure called a corneal debridement or grid keratotomy may be necessary to encourage healing. In severe cases of proptosis, immediate surgical replacement of the globe is required. Always follow your vet's prescription exactly.

The Role of Genetics: What Breeders Can Do

As an owner, you can also make a difference by supporting responsible breeding practices. Many eye conditions in Shih Tzus have a known genetic component. The Orthopedic Foundation for Animals (OFA) recommends that all breeding Shih Tzus undergo a CERF (Canine Eye Registration Foundation) examination annually by a board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist.

Understanding the OFA Database

When looking for a puppy, ask the breeder for OFA or CERF certification numbers for both parents. A breeder who cannot provide these may not be screening for hereditary cataracts, progressive retinal atrophy (PRA), or retinal dysplasia. A 2020 study cited by PubMed found that the prevalence of inherited cataracts in Shih Tzus was 3.5%, which is significant for a breed with so many other ocular risks. By choosing a breeder who screens, you reduce the risk of passing on preventable genetic defects.

Emergency Scenarios: When to Go to the Vet NOW

Some conditions cannot wait for a scheduled appointment. Knowing the difference between a routine problem and an emergency is life-saving. If you are unsure, always err on the side of caution and go to an emergency vet.

Signs of an Ocular Emergency

  • Sudden Proptosis: The eyeball is visibly displaced from the socket. Keep the eye moist with sterile saline or lubricating eye drops (never touch the eye itself) and transport your dog immediately. Every minute counts for saving the eye and preventing blindness.
  • Deep Corneal Ulcer: A deep, discolored, or "melting" ulcer (keratomalacia) requires surgical intervention. Look for a sudden increase in discharge and a white or greenish spot on the cornea.
  • Acute Glaucoma: The eye appears very red, the pupil is fixed and dilated, and the globe feels hard. This is intensely painful and leads to permanent blindness within 24-48 hours without treatment. This is a true emergency.
  • Sudden Blindness: If your dog bumps into walls or appears disoriented, it could indicate a retinal detachment, optic neuritis, or a stroke. Do not delay.

If you experience an eye emergency, do not administer any human eye drops (like Visine) as they can be toxic. Use only sterile eyewash or a lubricant prescribed for dogs. You can find our emergency contact protocols on our contact page.

Living with a Shih Tzu: A Long-Term Commitment to Eye Health

Owning a Shih Tzu is a 12-to-16-year commitment to proactive eye care. While the statistics are daunting, they are also manageable with knowledge and diligence. The breed’s popularity is well-deserved for their wonderful temperament and loyalty, but it comes with a responsibility to monitor their most sensitive feature daily.

Building a Routine

Integrate eye checks into your daily routine. While you are brushing their teeth or giving them a massage, take ten seconds to look at their eyes. Notice if they are bright and clear or if there is any redness, discharge, or squinting. Early detection is your best ally. A consistent routine is discussed further in our blog.

Costs and Insurance Considerations

Treating chronic Shih Tzu eye problems can be expensive. A single bout of proptosis surgery can cost $1,500 to $3,000 or more. Lifelong medications for Dry Eye can run $30-$60 per month. Consider investing in pet health insurance that specifically covers hereditary and chronic conditions. Read the fine print on pre-existing condition clauses, as Shih Tzu eye problems are so common that many insurance companies consider them breed-related.
