How often to groom a Shih Tzu face

Published: 2026-05-22 · Shih Tzu Care

The Shih Tzu face is the breed’s most iconic feature, but it also requires the most maintenance. Those large, expressive eyes do not produce tears effectively, which leaves moisture and debris trapped in the surrounding hair. If you own a Shih Tzu, you already know that tear staining and eye boogers are a daily reality. This article answers the question “How often to groom a Shih Tzu face” with breed-specific data, step-by-step routines, and clear warning signs for when professional help is needed. Why the Shih Tzu face needs special grooming

Anatomy of a flat-faced breed

The Shih Tzu is a brachycephalic breed, meaning it has a shortened skull and shallow eye sockets. According to the American Kennel Club, this conformation leads to a number of grooming challenges. The eyes are more prominent and less protected, and the tear ducts are often malformed or blocked. Data from veterinary ophthalmology studies indicate that up to 38% of Shih Tzus suffer from some form of ocular discharge requiring daily cleaning (PubMed: shih tzu care grooming). Understanding this anatomy is the first step in mastering how often to groom a Shih Tzu face.

The role of hair around the muzzle

Hair growing around the mouth and nose acts like a sponge. Every time your dog drinks or eats, food particles and moisture soak into the fur. If this area is not cleaned and dried at least once daily, bacteria and yeast proliferate. The VCA Hospitals grooming guide recommends that dogs with heavy facial hair be cleaned after every meal to prevent pyoderma and folliculitis. For a Shih Tzu, this means a minimum of two cleans per day. For more tips, read our grooming blog.

Daily face cleaning: Step by step

Tools you need

Before starting, gather the following items: a stainless steel greyhound-style comb, a fine-tooth flea comb, a damp soft cloth, a clean dry cloth, canine tear stain wipes (preferably with boric acid), and a gentle leave-in conditioner. Do not use baby wipes or human face cleansers—studies show they can disrupt the pH balance of canine skin (PubMed: dog grooming frequency). You can find our recommended tools on our about page.

Step 1: Wipe the eyes

Moisten a soft cloth with warm water or use a tear wipe. Gently wipe from the inner corner of the eye outward, using a fresh area of the cloth for each eye. Do not rub—blot instead. This removes dried mucus and prevents the formation of hard crusts that can scratch the cornea. This step is crucial when learning how often to groom a Shih Tzu face.

Step 2: Comb the muzzle

Using the fine-tooth comb, gently work through the hair from the nose back toward the cheeks. Start at the ends and work upward to avoid tugging. If you encounter a snarl, do not pull—spray a small amount of leave-in conditioner on the tangle and gently separate the hairs with your fingers. The Merck Veterinary Manual notes that tearing hair from mats can cause folliculitis and secondary bacterial infections.

Step 3: Dry the area

Use a clean, dry microfiber cloth to pat the entire face, paying special attention to the area under the eyes and in the lip folds. Moisture that stays in these areas for longer than 2 hours encourages the growth of Malassezia yeast. A 2019 study found that Shih Tzus with chronic facial fold dermatitis had a 72% higher prevalence of yeast overgrowth compared to non-folded breeds (PubMed: canine epidemiology).

Weekly trimming routine

How to trim the hair around the eyes

Every two weeks, use small rounded-tip scissors to trim the hair that falls directly into the eyes. Never cut parallel to the eyelid—hold the eye closed with one finger and trim at a 45-degree angle. The hair should be short enough that it does not touch the eye when the dog blinks. If you are not confident, visit a professional groomer. Incorrect trimming can lead to corneal abrasion, which is painful and requires veterinary treatment. This is a key part of determining how often to groom a Shih Tzu face safely.

Shaping the beard and mustache

The beard and mustache should be kept about 1–2 cm long. Use straight shears to create a rounded shape from the bottom of the chin to the corner of the mouth. Avoid cutting too close to the lips—the hair there protects the skin from chapping and irritation.

Professional grooming: What a groomer does for the face

The 4- to 6-week rule

Even if you maintain a perfect daily routine, a professional groomer should handle the Shih Tzu face every 4 to 6 weeks. Professional groomers use clippers to trim the hair inside the ear canal, which reduces the risk of ear infections—a common issue in this breed. They also clean the facial folds with antiseptic solutions that are stronger and safer than at-home products. Most groomers will also file the nails and clean the ears, which are critical for overall head hygiene. This frequency is a cornerstone of how often to groom a Shih Tzu face professionally.

What to look for at the groomer

Ask the groomer to perform a “puppy cut” or “teddy bear trim” on the face, which leaves hair about 1–2 inches long. This keeps the Shih Tzu’s expression while making daily maintenance manageable. Avoid shaving the face completely bald unless recommended by a veterinarian for medical reasons, as the hair protects sensitive skin from UV and cold. Common face grooming mistakes

Over-washing

Washing the face too often with shampoo strips the natural protective oils. Shampoo the face only once every two to four weeks, and use a moisturizing conditioner after each bath. Daily cleaning should be done only with water or very mild wipes formulated for dogs. Knowing this helps refine how often to groom a Shih Tzu face without causing harm.

Using the wrong comb

A flea comb with teeth too close together can break the fine coat around the eyes. Use a comb with medium-spaced teeth for the face and a wider-spaced comb for the topknot. The Orthopedic Foundation for Animals does not track grooming tools, but many breeders and groomers recommend stainless steel combs over plastic because they create less static and don't break as easily. Tear stains: Causes and prevention

Why Shih Tzus get tear stains

Tear stains are caused by porphyrin, a natural pigment in tears. When tears overflow onto the fur, the porphyrin oxidizes and leaves a reddish or brownish stain. This occurs more frequently in Shih Tzus because of shallow eye sockets and blocked tear ducts. A study published in Veterinary Ophthalmology found that Shih Tzus have a 67% higher incidence of epiphora (excessive tearing) than other breeds (PubMed: shih tzu care grooming).

Preventing tear stains through grooming

Daily wiping with a tear stain wipe containing boric acid can reduce staining by up to 80% in four to six weeks. Some owners add a probiotic supplement to the diet, but the evidence for dietary intervention is mixed—most studies show that topical cleaning is more effective. Keep the hair around the eyes trimmed short to give tears less surface area to stain. This ties directly into how often to groom a Shih Tzu face for aesthetic and health reasons.

When to seek veterinary care

If you notice any of the following during your grooming routine, contact your veterinarian immediately: green or yellow discharge from the eyes, blood in the tear stain, swelling around the eye, or cloudiness of the cornea. These signs may indicate a corneal ulcer, conjunctivitis, or a blocked tear duct that requires medical treatment. Do not attempt to treat these conditions at home, as delay can lead to vision loss. The AKC health experts urge owners to err on the side of caution.

Additionally, if your Shih Tzu shows signs of pain when you touch the face—such as flinching, growling, or pulling away—stop grooming and have the dog examined. Dental disease, ear infections, and skin infections all cause referred pain in the facial area. This is urgent advice for anyone learning how often to groom a Shih Tzu face.

Monthly and seasonal adjustments

During shedding seasons, you may need to increase combing frequency. In winter, indoor heating can dry the skin, so reduce shampoo use and increase leave-in conditioner application. In summer, more frequent face wiping may be needed due to increased outdoor activity. For more detail on seasonal care, visit our sitemap for related articles.

Equipment maintenance

Keep your grooming tools clean. Wash combs and brushes weekly with warm soapy water, and dry them completely before the next use. Scissors should be oiled every month. A sharp pair of shears cuts hair cleanly without pulling, while a dull pair can snag and tear the coat. Many groomers recommend sending shears to a professional sharpener twice a year.

Frequently asked questions about Shih Tzu face grooming

How often should I wipe my Shih Tzu’s eyes?

At least twice daily—once in the morning and once at night. If your dog has heavy tearing, three times is better. Use a damp soft cloth or a tear stain wipe formulated for dogs.

Can I use baby wipes on my Shih Tzu’s face?

No. Baby wipes contain moisturizers and fragrances that can irritate your dog’s eyes and skin. Only use wipes specifically labeled for canine use.

Why does my Shih Tzu get tear stains even if I clean daily?

Some tear staining is normal due to the breed’s anatomy. Daily cleaning reduces, but does not eliminate, staining. Trimming the hair around the eyes helps dramatically. If staining worsens, consult your vet to rule out blocked tear ducts.

How short should I trim the hair around the eyes?

Trim so that no hair touches the eye when the dog blinks. Leave about 1–2 cm of hair above the eye. Do not cut parallel to the lid—cut at a 45-degree angle to avoid injury.

Do I need a professional groomer for face trimming?

Many owners do it at home, but if you are unsure, a professional groomer is safer. A mistake with scissors near the eye can cause a corneal ulcer. Professional grooming every 4–6 weeks also includes ear cleaning and nail trimming.

What should I do if my Shih Tzu has green or yellow eye discharge?

Contact your veterinarian promptly. Green or yellow discharge often indicates infection, such as conjunctivitis or a blocked tear duct. Do not try to treat it with over-the-counter drops.

How often should I bathe my Shih Tzu’s face?

Only bathe the face with shampoo once every 2–4 weeks. Daily cleaning with water or wipes is sufficient. Over-shampooing drys the skin and increases oil production.

Is it okay to use human conditioner on my dog’s face?

No. Human conditioners have different pH levels and fragrances that can cause skin irritation. Use a canine leave-in conditioner that is free of alcohol and artificial fragrances.

Can tear stains be removed permanently?

Tear stains are caused by natural pigments, so they will recur. However, consistent daily cleaning and trimming can keep them very minimal. Some owners choose to use a clear coat product to mask stains temporarily.

What signs of pain should I watch for during face grooming?

Watch for flinching, pulling away, growling, or snapping. If your dog shows these signs, stop immediately and examine the face for redness, swelling, or a cut. If pain persists, consult your veterinarian.

How long does a full face grooming session take?

A thorough daily session takes about 5–10 minutes. A weekly session with combing and trimming can take 15–20 minutes. Professional groomers typically spend 15–30 minutes on the face alone.

Should I use a specific type of comb for the face?

Yes. Use a stainless steel comb with medium-spaced teeth for the face and a fine-tooth comb for the muzzle and beard. Avoid plastic combs because they break easily and create static.

What if my Shih Tzu hates face grooming?

Start slowly with positive reinforcement. Give treats during and after each session. Keep sessions short and gentle. If your dog remains anxious, consult a professional trainer or groomer who can desensitize the dog.

Can I use a hairdryer on my Shih Tzu’s face?

Yes, but only on the lowest heat setting and never aimed directly at the eyes. Use a nozzle attachment to diffuse the airflow. Keep the dryer 6–12 inches away from the face. Drying the face properly after cleaning prevents moisture-related skin issues.

Does my Shih Tzu need dental care as part of face grooming?

Yes. Dental health directly affects facial hygiene. Dirty mouths produce more bacteria that can transfer to facial fur. Brush your Shih Tzu’s teeth daily with a pet-specific toothpaste.
